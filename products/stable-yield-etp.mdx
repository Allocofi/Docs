Stable Yield ETPs are structured onchain products designed to pursue steadier return profiles through defined yield strategies. Each product is built around a specific yield-generating approach — such as funding rate capture, basis carry, or stablecoin yield — with transparent NAV, clear risk disclosures, and defined redemption terms. If you are looking for onchain exposure with a more consistent return target, Stable Yield ETPs provide a structured and transparent way to access those strategies.

## What is a Stable Yield ETP?

Stable Yield ETPs target yield-generating strategies where the return profile is expected to be more consistent relative to directional market exposure. The product rules define the eligible assets, yield calculation method, rebalancing schedule, and risk limits — so you always know what the product is doing and how its value is being calculated.

<Warning>
  The name "Stable Yield" does not imply principal protection, guaranteed yield, no risk or fixed return. Neutral strategies may lose money, arbitrage strategies may fail, and inflation-hedge products may not offset inflation.
</Warning>

## Strategy Types

Stable Yield ETPs may be built around a range of common yield-generating approaches:

- **Funding rate capture** — earning positive funding from perpetual contract markets where funding rates favour the long or short side
- **Basis carry** — exploiting price differences between spot and futures markets to capture the convergence spread
- **Onchain yield** — staking rewards, liquidity provision, or protocol-native yield from DeFi positions
- **Stablecoin strategies** — structured yield from stablecoin deployments across lending protocols or treasury instruments
- **Treasury-linked yield** — exposure to short-term rates through TradFi-linked structures such as T-bill wrappers or rate-linked notes

## Risk Considerations

Even though these products target steadier returns, all Stable Yield ETPs carry real risks. You should review the product documents carefully before investing. Key risks to understand include:

- Funding rates may reverse or basis spreads may widen unexpectedly
- Hedging instruments may fail or become unavailable during market stress
- Liquidity may disappear during stress events, affecting your ability to exit
- Model assumptions or correlations that underpin the strategy may break
- Execution costs — including gas, slippage, and rebalancing costs — may erode strategy returns
